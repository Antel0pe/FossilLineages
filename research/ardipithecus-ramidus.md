# Ardipithecus ramidus ("Ardi") — research note (2026-06-28)

## How it lived
- Pelvis/hip allowed upright walking, but long arms/fingers and an elongated lower pelvis kept
  it a capable forest climber — genuinely both, not "almost human."
- Carbon isotopes from teeth show a woodland-based, varied omnivore diet (fruit, nuts, maybe
  small vertebrates/eggs), not grassland grazing — lived and fed mostly under canopy.
- Striking near-absence of male-female canine size difference: unlike most apes, males weren't
  much bigger-fanged than females. The standard interpretation (Lovejoy) is reduced male-male
  canine threat-display, hinting at lower aggression between males and possibly more
  pair-bonding/cooperative group dynamics than typical ape societies — a real behavioral signal
  from a single tooth-size pattern, not guesswork from nothing.

## Why it diverged / pressures
- Bipedalism appears as a partial, "started but not finished" adaptation layered onto a fully
  forest-adapted body — consistent with patchy woodland food access being the driver, not open
  savanna.
- Reduced canine dimorphism (if Lovejoy's social read is right) suggests the social-behavior
  pressure and the locomotor pressure were moving together: less male-male canine competition
  may have freed up energy/risk budget that later let bipedal foraging and provisioning
  strategies take hold.
  - Source: Wikipedia/Britannica synthesis of White et al. 2009 Ardi monograph (Science).
