# Homo floresiensis ("the Hobbit") — research note (2026-06-28)

## How it lived
- Cut marks on dwarf Stegodon and giant-rat bones show active carcass processing — organised
  foraging, with systematic butchery patterns suggesting more than opportunistic scavenging,
  though hunting vs. scavenging can't be fully separated from the evidence.
- Toolkit (simple flakes, cores, choppers) resembles early Oldowan-style technology, surprising
  for such a late-surviving population — likely also made spears/traps for hunting.
- Tooth wear shows tough, fibrous chewing alongside the meat — a mixed, demanding diet on a
  resource-limited island.
- Shared the island with other dwarfed/giant fauna (dwarf Stegodon, giant rats, giant storks,
  big lizards) — a full island-rule ecosystem, not an isolated anomaly.

## Why it diverged / pressures
- Classic island dwarfism: long isolation + limited food + few predators favors smaller bodies
  (and likely brains) because a smaller body costs less to maintain on a resource-capped island.
- Notably, genetic work shows today's Flores pygmy people are NOT descended from this species —
  modern human dwarfing on the island happened independently, later. That means the same
  "island rule" pressure produced small-bodied hominins on Flores twice, from different
  starting lineages — strong evidence the pressure (not shared ancestry) is what's doing the work.
  - Source: NHM "real-life hobbit"; Haaretz (2018) on independent pygmy dwarfing; Liang Bua faunal/butchery studies.
