# Australopithecus afarensis ("Lucy") — research note (2026-06-28)

## How it lived
- 3D geometric morphometrics on fibulae (5 specimens vs. 40 living ape/human comparisons) put
  afarensis's ankle/tendon geometry closer to apes than humans — shorter tendon-attachment
  area, more ape-like malleolus shape. Net: real bipedalism, but biomechanically distinct from
  ours, with retained climbing capability.
- Laetoli footprints (3.66 Ma) confirm a real bipedal gait but with more flexed limbs at
  footstrike than modern humans — a different walking style, not "modern gait, just older." A
  2024 re-analysis found a second trackway at the same site with a *different* gait signature,
  plus two individuals walking side-by-side with synchronized steps — evidence of small-group
  coordinated movement.
- Carbon isotopes from Hadar/Dikika teeth (3.4 Ma) show wide individual dietary variation:
  0%-69% grass/sedge intake across individuals — some lived more forest-bound, others foraged
  more open ground, within the same population. Real ecological flexibility, not one fixed diet.

## Why it diverged / pressures
- Walking was established and refined before brain size grew — efficient ground travel between
  scattered, variable food patches mattered earlier in the timeline than cognitive expansion did.
- The population's dietary flexibility (some individuals more forest-feeding, others more
  open-ground) is plausibly what let two very different descendant strategies emerge from the
  same starting population: Paranthropus boisei doubled down on mechanical fallback-feeding
  while Homo habilis went the tool-assisted, higher-quality-food route — afarensis already
  contained the behavioral range that the next split exploited in opposite directions.
  - Source: PMC9314891 (fibula 3D-GM); Royal Society Laetoli gait studies + 2024 reanalysis; PNAS 2013 (Hadar/Dikika isotopes).
