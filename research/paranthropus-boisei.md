# Paranthropus boisei ("Nutcracker Man") — research note (2026-06-28)

## How it lived
- The skull screams hard-object feeding: massive flat molars, thick enamel, flaring
  cheekbones, sagittal crest for huge jaw muscles. But dental microwear texture analysis
  (confocal profilometry on 7 teeth) found fine striations, not the heavy pitting hard-object
  feeding leaves behind.
- This is "Liem's Paradox": the chewing machine was built for emergencies, not daily use.
  Conclusion — boisei probably ate soft food most days and only fell back on tough, hard,
  low-quality food during periods of scarcity, the same strategy modern gorillas use.
- So its anatomy reflects worst-case survival capacity, not typical day-to-day diet — a
  population built to survive lean seasons rather than one that "loved" hard food.

## Why it diverged / pressures
- Diverged from the afarensis-descendant population alongside Homo habilis at roughly the same
  time/place — two opposite bets on the same problem (variable, sometimes-scarce food): boisei
  invested in mechanical fallback capacity (bigger jaws/teeth to survive lean seasons directly),
  while habilis invested in tool use to access higher-quality food (meat, marrow) instead.
  boisei's strategy worked for over a million years before going extinct without descendants —
  a real "side branch" rather than a failure, just one that didn't continue.
  - Source: PLOS ONE 2008 (Ungar et al., dental microwear texture analysis, pone.0002044).
