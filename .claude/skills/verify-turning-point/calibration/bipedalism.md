# bipedalism — raw text as captured 2026-06-25

## Kicker + headline
> ~6 million years ago, somewhere in East Africa
> The forest is running out of trees.

## Scene-setting
> For millions of years, the climate has been drying. The unbroken canopy your ancestors
> moved through — branch to branch, never touching the ground — is breaking into islands:
> patches of fig and fruit trees separated by open, sun-scorched woodland.

## Voice 1 (the unadapted individual)
> We crawled through it the way we always do — the grass closing in over our backs, the
> trail ahead nothing but green for the next few strides and then nothing at all. Something
> moved wrong in it, once, off to the side. By the time any of us looked up, there were three
> of us where there had been four.
>
> The next time we crossed it, I tried standing up partway every few strides, just to see —
> straining up onto my back legs, slow, every muscle in my hips fighting where it isn't built
> to go, for half a breath before it gives out. Most strides that's all it is, half a breath
> of nothing. But once it was enough: a shape low and pale at the treeline, and something in
> my chest dropped — I was down and bolting away from it as fast as my legs would carry me.

## Time-skip + Voice 2 (the adapted individual)
> two million years later
>
> When the troop fans out across the next stretch of grass, I rise onto two legs without
> thinking about it, the way I always do, and keep going. To my left, some of the others are
> still down on their knuckles, already falling behind in the green.
>
> Off to my right, where the grass dips low, I catch movement before the others do —
> something quick and low, cutting through it. I move away from that side myself, and it
> isn't until a few minutes later that one of the knuckle-walkers near that same dip jerks
> upright, shrieks, and goes quiet. Whichever of them that was, they never saw it coming the
> way I did.
>
> At the fig tree we stay for hours, gorging until I can't fit another one in my mouth. When
> the troop finally turns back, I carry a last handful home as well, both hands free the
> whole way, while the others make do with what they can hold between their teeth, dropping
> pieces with every stride.

## Echo
> what's still with us
>
> The same narrowing of the pelvis that made upright walking efficient also narrowed the
> birth canal — one reason human childbirth is markedly longer, harder, and more dangerous
> than in any other living ape, who give birth quickly and largely unassisted. Anthropologists
> call this trade-off the "obstetric dilemma."

## Source note
> This scene dramatizes a real, debated transition. The fossil record (a bipedally loaded
> femur from Orrorin tugenensis, ~6 Ma; an upright-adapted pelvis and foot in Ardipithecus
> ramidus, ~4.4 Ma; a fully committed biped in Australopithecus afarensis, ~3.7–3 Ma) shows
> upright walking emerging gradually in increasingly open, patchy woodland. The advantages
> dramatized above draw on two leading, named hypotheses: Lovejoy's provisioning hypothesis
> (1981) — carrying food to mates/offspring — and Rodman & McHenry's locomotor-energetics
> hypothesis (1980) — bipedal walking's lower energy cost over distance. The improved
> predator-detection from standing taller is a longstanding, widely discussed companion idea,
> though harder to test directly than the other two. The childbirth trade-off is Washburn's
> obstetric dilemma hypothesis (1960). No single cause is proven; these are credible,
> evidence-backed candidates, not invented mechanism.

## User criticisms

**Kicker + headline** — Likes it. "Direct into the point," sets the scene immediately, "oh
shit" reaction. No notes.

**Scene-setting**
- Em-dash overuse reads as AI slop. Specifically: "the unbroken canopy your ancestors moved
  through — branch to branch, never touching the ground —" — flanking a clause in double
  em-dashes is the tell. Not a ban on em-dashes entirely, but this is overuse.
- Likes the rhythm: a short, snappy sentence ("For millions of years, the climate has been
  drying.") followed by a longer, descriptive one. Wants this contrast kept.
- Likes "patches of fig and fruit trees separated by open, sun-scorched woodland" — concrete,
  full-body, visceral image. This is the bar other sentences should be measured against.
- The jump from this third-person/narrator register straight into first-person "We crawled
  through it the way we always do" is jarring. Needs an explicit signal that we're now inside
  the animal's head — something parallel in function to the kicker line ("you're an
  Australopithecus" or similar), not just an unmarked register switch.

**Voice 1, paragraph 1 (the loss)**
- "the trail ahead nothing but green for the next few strides and then nothing at all" is
  weaker word choice than the scene-setting image — doesn't pop the same way. The intended
  feeling (you're below the grass, can't see, exposed, animal-on-the-ground-vulnerability) is
  the right target but this phrasing undersells it.
- This moment — "something moved wrong... three of us where there had been four" — is
  supposed to be the *worst-case realization of the vulnerability just established*: maximal
  exposure, can't see, used to trees, now on the ground. The visceral terror here should hit
  harder than it currently does.

**Voice 1, paragraph 2 (the rudimentary attempt)**
- Logical-plausibility concern: real chimps/apes can already stand briefly without much
  visible strain (just not balance well for long). Framing the attempt as "every muscle in my
  hips fighting" — i.e. a Herculean feat of strength — may not be biomechanically honest.
  Candidate alternative: frame the failure as a *balance* failure (swaying, toppling) rather
  than a *strength* failure.
- "every few strides, just to see" may also be a logical-flow problem: an animal traveling
  with purpose wouldn't pause repeatedly to experiment — that competes with the instinct to
  just get from A to B.
- Likes "most strides that's all it is, half a breath of nothing" — captures the
  fear-of-the-unseen well.
- Question (unresolved, not a verdict): is "a shape low and pale at the treeline" the right
  color/description for a savanna predator? Flag for a factual-accuracy pass, not just craft.
- Likes "I was down and bolting away from it as fast as my legs would carry me" — the payoff
  beat works, early detection leads directly to survival.
- Alternative idea floated (not mandated): a version where the rudimentary attempt narrowly
  *fails* instead of succeeds ("I didn't see it soon enough... it got me") — darker, would
  need separate evaluation, not a fixed requirement.

**Voice 2 (time-skip)**
- The time-skip paragraph lacks an establishing image/signal of "who you are now," parallel
  to what the opening kicker+headline did for voice 1. Consider whether each voice needs its
  own grounding image, not just the protagonist's first one.
- Suggests showing the adaptation progression through *quality of movement verbs*, not just
  declarative statement: e.g. voice 1 = crawling in trees → crawling on ground; voice 2 =
  clambering/bounding down → smoothly upright. "I rise onto two legs without thinking about
  it" tells rather than shows the contrast.
- Question (unresolved): is "already falling behind in the green" (knuckle-walkers vs.
  biped) scientifically/credibly accurate re: relative speed? Flag for fact-check, not just
  craft.
- **"I catch movement before the others do"** and **"I move away from that side myself"**
  read as *retrospective narration* — told after the fact with hindsight framing, rather than
  lived in the moment as it happens. This is a distinct failure mode from "explaining why":
  it's a tense/voice problem, not a content problem. Wants present-tense, in-the-moment,
  visceral phrasing instead (e.g., heart dropping, angling away *as it's happening*) — the
  "shark in the water" feeling, not a calm summary.
- Open logical question, unresolved: is it plausible that the protagonist sees danger, moves
  away without signaling/warning anyone, and a nearby individual dies shortly after — without
  it reading as implausibly callous or as a non-sequitur? No fixed verdict, but flagged as a
  case the skill should be able to interrogate (could also be resolved by putting the second
  individual's death further away/later, weakening the direct A-survives/B-dies comparison;
  trade-off is the trade-off is less obviously bipedalism's payoff if it's too far removed).
- **"Whichever of them that was, they never saw it coming the way I did."** — confirmed bad:
  too on-the-nose, states the evolutionary comparison directly instead of letting the reader
  connect the dots from the juxtaposed actions. The scene should paint the picture and let the
  point become obvious without narrating it. (This independently matches what a verify-pass
  flagged as a criterion 4/5 failure — strong signal this is a real, repeatable failure mode,
  not a one-off nitpick.)
- Fig-tree paragraph lacks connective tissue to the death moment right before it — feels like
  a hard cut. Consider establishing *why* the troop is moving/crossing at all earlier (food
  scarcity in the home patch, hunger) so arriving at the fig tree and gorging has earned
  narrative weight, and so the urgency of the original crossing (in voice 1 too) is motivated
  rather than arbitrary.
- "both hands free the whole way while the others make do with what they can hold between
  their teeth" again risks being too on-the-nose about explaining the advantage — same
  concern as the "never saw it coming" line, lower confidence. Needs the earlier
  motivation-setup (above) so the carrying detail lands as a natural consequence rather than
  a demonstration.
- Question (unresolved): why is the troop "turning back" — is the destination/home
  established clearly enough for this to make sense, or does it need setup?

**Echo / "what's still with us"**
- No voice-specific concerns — this section isn't tied to either voice. The bar here is
  simply: is the fact genuinely non-obvious/surprising ("oh, that's interesting") rather than
  common knowledge or something the reader already expects. That reaction is the pass
  condition.

**Recurring/general patterns observed across this story (candidates for SKILL.md
synthesis — proposed, not yet merged):**
1. Em-dash overuse (specifically the flanking-double-em-dash construction) is a concrete,
   checkable AI-tell — more specific than the current "AI-tell prose" criterion's vague
   guidance.
2. "Retrospective narration" — sentences that summarize an event with hindsight framing
   ("I caught it before the others did," "I moved away myself") instead of rendering it as it
   happens in the moment — is a distinct failure mode from "forced fact-squeezing." Both can
   make a sentence read as told-after-the-fact rather than lived, but retrospective narration
   is about *tense/immediacy*, not about *naming the evolutionary point*.
3. The "never saw it coming the way I did" pattern (criterion 4 in the existing skill) is
   confirmed as a real, repeatable failure — independently flagged by both the user and a
   verify-pass agent on the same sentence.
4. Logical/biomechanical plausibility of the rudimentary attempt itself (not just its
   narrative payoff) is a check the skill doesn't currently make explicit — e.g., is the
   *kind* of difficulty described (strength vs. balance) consistent with what's
   biomechanically plausible for the species at this stage.
5. Motivation/connective tissue between beats (why is the troop moving, why turn back, why
   does arriving somewhere matter) is currently un-checked — beats can read as disconnected
   even when each one individually passes the other 7 criteria.
6. Possible need for a per-voice grounding image/signal, not just one at the very top of the
   piece, so each voice's transition into first-person is clearly marked.
