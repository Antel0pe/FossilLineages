# endurance-running — raw text as captured 2026-06-25

## Kicker + headline
> ~1.8 million years ago, East African savanna, near midday
> By midday, the chase always gives out before the prey does.

## Scene-setting
> By late morning the grass empties out. The big cats have already dragged themselves into
> whatever shade still holds together, flanks heaving, done for hours. The antelope herds
> thin too, drifting toward the tree line, leaving the open ground to whoever can stand to
> cross it. Thick-coated, breathing hard within minutes of any real effort, the troop has
> never been able to use that emptiness for anything.

## Voice 1 (the unadapted individual)
> One of us went down in the grass before the kudu did, folded over her own knees, and didn't
> get back up for the rest of the day. We left her in what shade we could find and kept
> moving without her, the gap between us and the kudu opening again with every stride.
>
> The next time the herd scattered at midday, I went after the limping one myself, breath
> sawing, skin gone slick under matted hair that wouldn't let any of the heat out. My legs
> wanted to stop a hundred times before I let them. Twice I dropped to a walk, chest heaving,
> the edges of everything swimming — and twice the kudu ahead of me slowed to almost nothing
> in the same stride, head low, sides working like a bellows. The second time I closed half
> the gap before my own legs folded under me in the dirt, and the kudu staggered on into the
> trees without me.

## Time-skip + Voice 2 (the adapted individual)
> two hundred thousand years later
>
> Around me the rest of the band peels off into whatever shade the acacias offer, chests
> heaving, already done for the day. I keep moving. Off to the south, a jackal that's been
> pacing the same kudu falls out of the chase entirely, slinking into a thorn thicket, tongue
> lolling sideways out of its mouth.
>
> By the time the kudu's legs give out under it, mouth foaming, flanks heaving against ground
> gone hot enough to blister bare skin, I'm barely breathing hard. Sweat runs freely down my
> bare arms and drips off my chin into the dust. My hands close around a foreleg before it can
> find the strength to rise again.

## Echo
> what's still with us
>
> Per kilogram of muscle, no other land mammal sheds heat through sweat as efficiently as a
> human. A horse, run hard enough for long enough in the heat of the day, will eventually have
> to stop and cool down — a sweating human, on the same hot ground, often doesn't have to. In
> Wales's annual Man versus Horse Marathon, on hot years, human runners have beaten every horse
> in the field.

## Source note
> This scene dramatizes a real, debated hypothesis about why humans became such unusual
> long-distance runners. Lieberman and Bramble's endurance-running hypothesis (2004, Nature)
> identifies the specific traits that appear together in Homo erectus (~1.9–0.1 Ma) and
> persist in humans today: an enlarged gluteus maximus and a nuchal ligament that stabilize
> the trunk and head during running, a long Achilles tendon and arched foot that store and
> return elastic energy, and a dramatic increase in eccrine sweat glands paired with loss of
> body fur for evaporative cooling. Persistence hunting itself is not pure conjecture: Louis
> Liebenberg documented modern San hunters in the Kalahari running game down to heat
> exhaustion without weapons, the same way described above, as a real-world analog for how the
> ancestral behavior likely worked. No single hypothesis is proven beyond debate, but these are
> credible, evidence-backed traits and behaviors, not invented mechanism.

## User criticisms

**Kicker** — fine.

**Headline** — "By midday, the chase always gives out before the prey does." Doesn't land on
first read; the reader has to stop and work out who's chasing whom and why. Contrast with the
bipedalism headline ("The forest is running out of trees"), which is instantly legible.
Candidate replacement direction: state the plain background problem first — our prey is
always faster than us, we can't catch it, food is running low — not a clever-but-oblique
restatement of the conclusion. This is a *different* failure from sentence-level readability
(criterion 7): the sentence parses grammatically fine, but the *situation* it describes
doesn't register immediately. Headline is "the single most load-bearing line," so it should
be held to an even higher immediacy bar than body prose.

**Scene-setting**
- Specific framing around "midday" feels like a superfluous detail unless the hypothesis is
  specifically about midday hunting (it might be — Lieberman/Bramble is about heat broadly,
  not necessarily strictly noon) — flag for fact-check on whether the midday specificity is
  warranted by the actual hypothesis, vs. just generic heat-exhaustion framing being the real
  point.
- "leaving the open ground to whoever can stand to cross it" read as wordy/superfluous, not
  adding to the picture.
- This beat should function like a documentary's wide establishing shot: the big, broad
  world-state picture (food is low, the prey is always faster, we can't catch it) before any
  specifics — not yet emotionally vivid (that's voice 1's job) but should clearly state the
  background problem. Wants more of this broad-strokes problem statement here, with the
  vivid/visceral version (sweat, suffocating heat, near-collapse) saved for voice 1.
- Image used reads as too hairless for this point in the timeline — looks closer to a modern
  human than the species should be. Flagged as a possible science/visual-accuracy issue, but
  also raised an open question: is it even biomechanically/evolutionarily plausible for a
  species to be comfortably bipedal+endurance-running-capable while *not yet* hairless enough
  to avoid heat exhaustion — i.e., would hair loss and endurance-running capability have
  evolved in lockstep, making "hairy but already a good runner" an inconsistent combination?
  Unresolved — flagged for fact-check, not a craft verdict. (Out of this skill's current
  scope, which only reads prose — note as a process gap, see below.)

**Voice 1, paragraph 1 (the loss)**
- "Kudu" is an obscure species name to introduce here, and inconsistent with "antelope" used
  one beat earlier — should be a recognizable term, used consistently.
- "didn't get back up for the rest of the day" reads as ambiguous/implausible — sounds like
  total physical collapse (and on the savanna that reads as fatal), but the next sentence
  ("we left her in what shade we could find") implies she's merely resting somewhere, not
  collapsed in place. The two images don't reconcile; this is a continuity/clarity problem,
  not just word choice.
- Wants a capping image showing the kudu actually getting away at the end of this paragraph
  (e.g., "until it was but a dot on the horizon") — currently ends on "the gap opening with
  every stride," which doesn't fully land the failure.

**Voice 1, paragraph 2 (the rudimentary attempt)**
- "The next time the herd scattered at midday" repeats the unresolved midday-specificity
  question and introduces a "which herd" ambiguity (kudu vs. antelope vs. an unspecified
  herd).
- Likes: "the edges of everything swimming," "sides working like a bellows," "skin gone slick
  under matted hair that wouldn't let any of the heat out" (though this last one is right on
  the line of being too on-the-nose/strained — flag as borderline, not a clean pass).
- "I went after the limping one myself, breath sawing, skin gone slick" introduces full
  exhaustion symptoms immediately, with no buildup — there's no established prior state of
  "I had been running for a while" before symptoms appear. This is a **continuity gap**: full
  exhaustion appearing instantly, un-earned, rather than escalating from a clear starting
  point.
- "Twice I dropped to a walk" implies a prior state of running that was never explicitly
  established in the text — same continuity-gap pattern as above: a later sentence assumes an
  earlier state the reader was never shown.
- "wanted to stop a hundred times" — the specific number reads as an unnatural, slightly
  cliché turn of phrase; flagged as something that could likely be improved without being a
  fixed rule.
- General direction (not mandatory, but worth capturing as a candidate model for "the
  rudimentary attempt" pacing): a persistence hunt should probably show **gradual escalation
  over real time** — losing sight of the prey, catching up again, the prey's recovery time
  visibly shrinking each cycle, ending with the prey's collapse and the protagonist's near
  -collapse together — rather than a single compressed exchange. The general principle: a
  multi-stage physical ordeal shouldn't be compressed into one paragraph if compression costs
  the reader the sense of duration/gradual escalation that makes the eventual payoff earned.

**Voice 2 (time-skip)**
- The jackal paragraph doesn't add value and its appearance is unmotivated/unclear — distracts
  from this beat's actual job, which is showing the *adapted-vs-unadapted* contrast within the
  same species.
- Strong preferred alternative: show the contrast via a **directly comparable companion in the
  same scene** — another individual, visibly less-adapted (hairier, sweating more / overheating
  faster), running alongside the protagonist and then dropping out partway — rather than an
  abstract/unrelated animal (the jackal) or an offstage "others are falling behind" statement.
  This is a stronger, more concrete version of the existing skeleton's "make the population
  split visible in the same moment" requirement — the comparison should be a live, direct,
  side-by-side observation, not an incidental aside.
- Open fact-check question: does a hairier individual actually sweat less effectively, or is
  it more about hair blocking evaporation/heat dissipation regardless of sweat output? Flag
  for verification, not a craft note.
- Wants voice 2's hunt to mirror voice 1's hunt structurally (so the reader feels the
  parallel) but with a clearly different *felt experience* — still some fatigue, but lighter,
  cooler, the same strategy now working smoothly where it barely worked before — rather than
  reusing identical imagery.
- General, recurring note across this whole story: too many sentences require the reader to
  pause and actively work out what's happening, rather than instantly picturing it. This
  shows up at the conceptual level (the headline, "which herd") as much as the sentence level.

**Echo**
- Likes both facts — the general "humans shed heat efficiently via sweat" claim and especially
  the specific Man vs. Horse Marathon detail ("very, very good"). No changes wanted here.
  Reinforces the existing pass bar: the fact should make the reader think "huh, that's
  interesting," not something already commonly known.

**Recurring/general patterns observed across this story (candidates for SKILL.md
synthesis — proposed, not yet merged):**
1. **Conceptual immediacy, distinct from sentence-level readability.** A sentence can parse
   grammatically (criterion 7 pass) while still failing to convey *what's happening* without
   the reader pausing to work it out (this story's headline; "which herd"). This deserves to
   be either its own criterion or an explicit sub-check under criterion 7/headline craft —
   "does the reader get the situation on first pass, not just the grammar."
2. **Scene-setting should be a wide establishing shot of the broad background problem**
   (prey is faster, food is low) — emotionally vivid imagery is voice 1's job, not
   scene-setting's. Scene-setting failing by being too vivid/specific wasn't previously a
   check; failing by being flatly expository was already covered, but failing by drowning the
   broad picture in superfluous specific detail is a new angle.
3. **Continuity gap: a later sentence assuming an earlier state never shown** ("twice I
   dropped to a walk" with no prior running established; full exhaustion appearing instantly
   with no escalation). Related to, but distinct from, the existing "no effect before its
   cause" check in the writing skill — this is "missing setup," not "wrong order."
4. **Direct, side-by-side comparison device for the time-skip voice** is stronger than an
   abstract/offstage "others are falling behind" — confirms and sharpens the existing
   skeleton's "population split visible in the same moment" rule with a concrete preferred
   form: a comparable companion in the same scene, not an unrelated animal or a vague aside.
5. **Species/terminology consistency** (kudu vs. antelope) — not previously checked.
6. **Scope gap:** several criticisms here are about scientific/biomechanical plausibility of
   the *depicted mechanism itself* (hairy-but-bipedal timing, hair vs. sweat efficiency) and
   about the *chosen image* — neither is currently in this skill's scope (SKILL.md setup step
   3 explicitly says to ignore JSX/images and extract prose only). Open question for later:
   should verify-turning-point gain an explicit "internal biological plausibility" check, and/or
   should image selection be evaluated by a different skill/pass entirely?
